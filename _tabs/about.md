Hi! I'm **Nikil Paul**, a software developer (for now) from India.

I spend most of my time taking things apart—not because they're broken, but because I want to understand **why** they were built that way.

My interests drift somewhere between Linux, distributed systems, infrastructure, networking, open source, and the occasional rabbit hole that starts with a simple question and somehow ends with me reading kernel documentation at 2 AM, wondering whether relying on AI for everything is slowly eating my ability to think.

I didn't get into software the "traditional" way. I jumped straight into building things, which meant I could make software long before I really understood the layers underneath it. It worked, but it also left me with a pretty shaky foundation.

For a while, AI helped fill those gaps. It let me move quickly, experiment, and build projects I probably wasn't ready for. I'm genuinely grateful for that. But somewhere along the way I realized that getting an answer isn't the same as understanding it.

These days, I'm trying to reverse that process.

Instead of asking _"How do I make this work?"_, I'm trying to ask _"Why does this work at all?"_

That shift has taken me down a very different path: operating systems, networking, filesystems, compilers, distributed systems, and eventually the Linux kernel.

Lately I've been contributing to Debian's infrastructure through **Debusine**, learning systems programming, and slowly working my way down the software stack—one layer at a time.

This blog is where I document that journey.

## Learning by breaking things

I have a habit of learning by pulling on threads until something unravels.

Sometimes that's productive.

Sometimes it's... less so.

At one point I deleted the **systemd user** because I wanted to see what it actually did. I learned exactly what it did—by ending up with a leaking D-Bus session, permission issues everywhere, and several hours of questioning my life choices.

Ended up reinstalling my OS because this wasn't something the current Nikil to figure out.

I could have read the documentation first.

But I also learned far more from fixing the mess than I probably would have otherwise.

That tends to be my learning style.

## My memory is terrible

I have an absolutely terrible memory.

I forget command-line flags.

I forget configuration options.

I forget where a file is supposed to live.

Sometimes I even forget the command I used yesterday.

You'll often find me with half a dozen documentation tabs open because I can't remember whether the flag was `-a`, `-A`, `--all`, or something completely different.

I've stopped seeing that as a weakness.

It just means I spend a lot of time reading documentation—which, in hindsight, is probably a good habit to build anyway.

## Learning with less AI

I'm still a fan of AI, and I still use it.

But I'm trying to change *how* I use it.

Instead of asking it to explain everything, I'm trying to let it point me toward the right documentation, standards, RFCs, source code, or beginner books.

I'm learning to become comfortable with not understanding something immediately.

To read the docs.

To read the man pages.

To read the source.

To read the beginner book before the advanced blog post.

It's slower.

Sometimes frustrating.

But I think it's making me a better engineer.

## I don't claim to be an expert

Most articles here are simply notes from someone learning in public.

If I discover a better approach tomorrow, I'll probably come back and update today's post. Understanding evolves, and I like leaving a trail of how my thinking changed.

One thing you'll notice is that I ask **"why?"** a lot.

> Why does this protocol exist?
>
> Why was this abstraction introduced?
>
> Why does the kernel do it this way?
>
> Why is the obvious solution not the one everyone uses?

Those questions tend to send me down interesting rabbit holes.

Outside of computers, I'm usually reading, listening to people who know far more than I do, or trying to convince everyone that cats are, in fact, overrated.

If you spot a mistake, have a better explanation, or just want to nerd out about systems, feel free to reach out.

Those conversations are half the reason this blog exists.
